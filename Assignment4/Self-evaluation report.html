Accessibility highlights section

The WCAG guidelines cover a wide range of instructions that focus on improving the overall user experience and providing higher accessibility to the Web content. Some of the guidelines that we have analyzed in the context of our website were related to:

  • accessibility from a different environment (adaptable for people that intend on viewing the content in a different form from the primary existing);
  • clear and meaningful structure and use of all additional methods of conveying information (with alternative to those that can view such additional methods);
  • Easy navigability through the site with higher focus and more direct access to the primary content of the site.

For analyzing applicability of all guidelines individually to the context of our site we have started first with checking if the specific guideline has significant relevance to the content of our site. So we have determined that the cases where the guidelines are related to audio content are not applicable to our site. Secondly, we identified the exact purpose of the guideline, and we have tried understanding if it matches the situations in which the content is used on our site. From the list of principles on WCAG we identified 3 clear guidelines that we think clearly apply to our webpage:

  • 1.3 Adaptable: It is necessary for our site to be accessible on different screens, user agents and environments for all users, as many shoppers will not navigate through the content of the store using both visual or aural presentation at the same time. This means that we need to provide a simpler layout for showing the products in different categories and occurrences, without losing any information or structure for the cases where users might use a screen reader. Furthermore, the content should preserve its structure when providing alternative presentation. The improvement of accessibility followed by this guideline can reflect the interest of customers to learn and memorise the pattern of operations needed to successfully purchase products from our site.
  • 1.4 Distinguishable: It is crucial for us that the content of our page is easy to handle without any constraints of using only color for conveying information. This guideline points to the idea that adapting the content not to be dependably on color can enable more users to find products (possibly purchase them) and therefore enlarge the customer base on our site. Furthermore, the color on our site is used only to improve the aesthetics. As previously stated, we have not integrated any audio content, thus the existence of audio control mechanism is not necessary.
  • 2.4 Navigable: It is important for our page to be easily navigated, and provide the most efficient and comfort to the users when they are trying to look for a specific section of the page. All sections require proper title. Given the fact that the nature of our website is e-commerce, there might be cases where certain products are promoted or displayed as block elements appearing after the whole page loads, or simple advertisement methods. In such cases it is important not to overlook the users that will be viewing the site using different user agents. Including section titles and page titles can decrease the time needed for users to find specific products or view other relevant products. For the case where new items are added to the top selling items table, the focus order plays an important role, since it enables navigating through input fields easily, without jumping to other sections of the page.

Guideline Compliance Description
1.1 Fails to comply 1.1.1 Non-text Content: Most of the content on our site is conveyed in a text form, with the exception of few images. However, the guideline clearly states that there is exemption of the content that is used for “decoration”, and as we have provided the image under the navigation bar that serves for such purposes, it clearly has an exemption. However, for every other case of non-text content, the situation of products listed in tables, we have not provided sufficient text alternatives to images, as the products were dynamically generated by user input. Therefore we do not comply fully with this guideline.(Level A)
1.3 Partially complies 1.3.1 Info and Relationships: Based on the “understanding” section of the guideline, it implies that information and relationships that are implied by visual or auditory formatting are preserved when the presentation format changes. Given the fact that we have used HTML structure only for semantics (not decoration or appearance), all content should retain its visual relationship in case of style sheet substitution. In terms of responsiveness of the content when viewed on a different screen, we have had some issues with media query at some screen size, and it was directly related to this guideline, as it disrupted the information and relationship between content. Overall, the structure and relationship of the content of our site did ensure that information important for comprehension will be perceivable to all. (Level A)
1.3.2 Meaningful Sequence: As the guideline “success criterion” states, the intent is to provide an alternative presentation of content while preserving reading order needed to understand the meaning. This could translate to providing an adequate print view of the content, or creating responsive design for the cases when content is viewed on different screens. By analyzing the site we have built, we have provided a somewhat sufficient print and responsive interface. However, there was some TA feedback suggesting that we had some issues with the media queries in CSS, and those issues were related to preserving reading order needed to understand the meaning. The print view had issues with the placement and viewing of the “about us” section. (Level A)
1.3.3 Sensory Characteristics:This guideline is related to any content that uses shape, size, spatial location or orientations (or any other visual cue) for navigation and the use of the website. Such content needs to have adequate text alternatives or information explaining the actions. As for our site, there is no content that uses any of the above mentioned attributes for accessibility purposes. Therefore we do comply with this part of the guideline.
1.4 Fully complies 1.4.1 Use of Color: As the guideline suggests, all information conveyed by using color (meaningfully) should have some form of alternative for the case where the user might not perceive color difference. In the case of our site, the use of colors heavily relies on the aesthetic of the site, thus there are no cases where particular meaningful information is provided only using color differences. There is only a case where color change is triggered using CSS hover or focus effect indicating the use of a particular element or input form. For such cases we have not provided any alternative information as it seems redundant. When it comes to paragraph sections, the colors are set to provide the contrast from the background, and therefore improve readability. (Level A)
1.4.2 Audio Control: This guideline is related to audio content, and as we do not have any it is not applicable. (Level A)
2.1 Fully complies 2.1.1 Keyboard: The intent of this guideline is to ensure that all content can be operated through the keyboard or keyboard interface. As we have only used simple HTML content (without any interactive graphics, integrated web application), we have relied on the browser's integrated keyboard accessibility. The site can be navigated using keyboard arrow keys, and all hyperlinks can be accessed using tab key and (shift + tab keys). (Level A)
2.1.2 No Keyboard Trap: The guideline explanations suggest that keyboard traps are a common problem when multiple formats are combined within a page and rendered using plug-ins or embedded applications. As previously stated in the guideline above, the contents of our site are HTML elements, and thus we have not encountered any “keyboard traps” while navigating throughout the site using the keyboard interface. (Level A)
2.4 Fully complies 2.4.1 Bypass Blocks: The intent of this guideline is to allow users more direct access to the primary content of the web page, by bypassing blocks of content that are repeated on multiple web pages. From the context of the guideline, types of such content could be advertisements, additional screens or block elements that are not related to the primary content. Given the fact that we do not have any non-related or repeating content we can consider saying that we comply or the guideline is not applicable to our site.
2.4.2 Page Titled: This guideline ensures that the users are able to find content and orient themselves within the site by requiring descriptive titles for the content. By having the HTML title, navigation bar, and heading for every section of our site we fully comply with this guideline.
2.4.3 Focus Order: The success criterion for this guideline ensures that navigation through site is consistent with the meaning of the content and can be operated from the keyboard. By analyzing the navigation on our site with a keyboard interface, we have noticed that the navigation follows sequential navigation. To further illustrate, the movement from the top of the page to the button using tab key does not skip any links that are present, and follows an ordered pattern. However, the movement through “explore popular products by categories” follows the HTML order, meaning that it jumps to the second column after finishing the list items from the first. When it comes to input form for adding new items to the table, every input field is triggered following the visible pattern.
2.4.4 Link Purpose (In Context): Based on the guideline explanation, every hyperlink should be understandable, allowing users to decide whether they want to navigate to it. We have provided clear meaning, purpose and destination for every hyperlink present on our site, thus there are no unclear actions behind pressing any link element. Thus we comply with this part of the guideline.

Overview of the improvements

Our approach to improvements is based on assessing the site as a whole and understanding which of its components are affecting the usability and accessibility of the site. As we have analyzed the WCAG guidelines and the TA feedback, we came to an idea of devising a list of issues organized by the source of the information. Afterward we worked on creating a list with prioritized improvements and execution plans, where every element of the list addresses all issues specified in Appendixes A and B. Starting from Appendix A, the guidelines that we have failed to comply with are directly related to the responsiveness, alternative view of the site and preserving the structure of the site while being presented in alternative viewing modes.

  • Firstly, there was a particular non-compliance issue related to providing alternative text description for non-text elements. This was caused by the fact that images presented in the “Top selling item” table were dynamically generated from the database, and the HTML “alt” (image alternative) attribute has been overlooked while developing the Javascript function that rendered new items to the table.
  • We haven’t also managed to retain the meaningful structure by creating a responsive design that adapts to all screens sizes. By inspecting clearly the style sheet (CSS) we identified the problem with the table, where table data does not resize appropriately and causes elements on the site to collide.
  • Final issue with guideline compliance was a print view, where the structure and presentation of the document was out of proportion and also the coloring of the text once in print view was not appropriate.

Moving to Appendix B, some of the improvement suggestions were related to technical implementation, that does not directly correlate with usability of accessibility aspects of the site. Cases of such feedback were using style-oriented markup in Javascript, not utilizing all CSS attributes (or using shorthand versions).
